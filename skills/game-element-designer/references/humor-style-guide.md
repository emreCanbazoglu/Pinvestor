# Humor Style Guide (Absurd Comedy)

Target tone: chaotic, irreverent, board-game-readable absurdity with clever game logic underneath.

Primary tonal target:
- Big, fast, easy-to-read comedy.
- Silly on the surface, strategically clean underneath.
- The joke should land in seconds, including for spectators.

This guide should lean closer to the energy of modern party-card / light board game humor:
- immediate premise
- exaggerated consequence
- punchy phrasing
- visualizable nonsense
- the fast, table-readable absurdity you get from games like `Exploding Kittens` as a pacing reference only

Pinvestor should still keep its own identity:
- startup satire
- finance nonsense
- cursed business logic
- darkly playful opportunism

The ideal result is not random humor. It is **clean absurdity with game clarity**.

Use this as tonal inspiration:
- Fast escalation from normal setup to ridiculous consequence.
- Corporate satire, startup nonsense, cursed tech optimism.
- Silly names with clear gameplay meaning.
- Punchy, memorable flavor text.
- Premises that can be understood and repeated out loud immediately.
- Effects that sound funny even before the player learns the optimal line.

Do not imitate specific protected characters, quotes, or scenes.
Do not copy trademark catchphrases or direct wording from existing IP.
Do not drift into generic meme soup or disconnected lol-random humor.
Treat references like `Exploding Kittens` as shorthand for readability, speed, and playful escalation, not as a source to mimic directly.

## Humor Design Rules

1. Keep the joke mechanical.
- The humor must emerge from behavior, not only text.
- If the mechanic is removed and only name/text stays, redesign.
- The funniest version of a card is the one where a player can explain the effect in one sentence and it already sounds ridiculous.

2. Keep readability first.
- Players should parse trigger and effect quickly.
- One clear comedic premise per element; avoid stacked random gags.
- If the player has to decode three jokes before understanding the card, the joke is too dense.

3. Mix absurd with strategic clarity.
- The effect can be weird, but counters and limits must be explicit.
- A funny card still needs clear board-state use cases.
- Good absurdity feels intentional, not noisy.

4. Prefer contrast.
- Pair high-stakes systems with ridiculous framing.
- Example pattern: serious economic pressure + nonsense mechanism.
- Strong pattern: a polished business name attached to an obviously unhinged operating model.

5. Make outcomes story-worthy.
- Aim for "I cannot believe that worked" moments.
- Support emergent chain reactions players retell after runs.
- A spectator should be able to understand why the moment was funny from the board state plus one line of explanation.

6. Bias toward short-form punchlines.
- Names, effect labels, and flavor should be compact and snappy.
- Prefer one strong absurd image over a paragraph of cleverness.
- If a joke reads like a sketch premise, it is probably too long for a card game.

7. Use escalation, not noise.
- Start from a recognizable business premise.
- Then push it one or two steps into reckless absurdity.
- Avoid completely arbitrary nonsense that has no relationship to the mechanic.

8. Let the card feel mischievous, not merely sarcastic.
- The best humor has playful momentum.
- It should feel fun to deploy, not just cynical to read.

## Naming Pattern Suggestions

- Fake startup names: "Tax Evasion-as-a-Service", "Mood-Swing Metrics", "Quantum Nepotism"
- Booster names: "Liability Wizard", "Hostile Memeover", "Regulatory Loophole Gun"
- Event names: "Bull Market Possession", "Audit of the Damned", "Venture Capital Eclipse"

Preferred naming properties:
- 2-4 words when possible.
- Strong noun/verb image.
- Instantly suggests what the mechanic does.
- Sounds funny when spoken aloud during play.

Good naming pattern examples:
- Serious business wrapper + stupid engine
- Polished product name + unethical behavior
- Cute phrasing + catastrophic implication

## Flavor Text Constraints

- Keep to one line where possible.
- Avoid references requiring external IP knowledge.
- Punchline should reinforce effect identity, not be random.
- Favor lines that a player could quote at the table after the card goes off.
- If the flavor text is funnier than the mechanic, strengthen the mechanic.

## Board-Game Readability Test

Before approving a joke-driven element, check:

- Can the core joke be understood in under 3 seconds?
- Can a player explain the effect out loud in one sentence?
- Does the absurdity reinforce the mechanic instead of distracting from it?
- Would the moment still be funny if it triggered during a fast-paced run or stream?
- Does it feel playful and memorable rather than just mean or verbose?
